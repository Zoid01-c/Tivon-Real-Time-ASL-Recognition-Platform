Understanding American Sign Language

Discover the rich visual language that bridges communication gaps and empowers the Deaf community

What is ASL?

American Sign Language (ASL) is a complete, natural language that has the same linguistic properties as spoken languages. It is expressed by movements of the hands and face, and is the primary language of many North Americans who are deaf and hard of hearing.

ASL is a visual language with its own grammar, syntax, and vocabulary distinct from English. It incorporates facial expressions, body postures, and spatial relationships to convey meaning and emotion.

History of ASL

ASL originated in the early 19th century at the American School for the Deaf (ASD) in Hartford, Connecticut. It evolved from a combination of French Sign Language (LSF) and various local sign languages used by deaf communities in America.

Thomas Hopkins Gallaudet and Laurent Clerc played pivotal roles in establishing the foundation of ASL education in America. Their work led to the establishment of numerous schools for the deaf and the widespread adoption of ASL as the primary language of the American Deaf community.

Structure and Grammar

ASL has its own grammar that differs from English. Signs are produced by combining specific handshapes, movements, and positions. The language uses facial expressions and body postures as grammatical markers, similar to how spoken languages use tone and inflection.

Key components of ASL include handshapes (similar to letters in spoken languages), movement (how the hands move), location (where the sign is made), palm orientation, and non-manual markers (facial expressions and body posture).

Key Facts

  • ASL is not a universal language - different countries have their own sign languages
  • ASL has regional variations and dialects, just like spoken languages
  • Facial expressions are a crucial part of ASL grammar
  • ASL is recognized as a complete and natural language

Learning Benefits

  • Enhances visual-spatial skills and cognitive abilities
  • Improves communication with the Deaf community
  • Opens career opportunities in interpretation and education
  • Promotes inclusivity and cultural awareness
